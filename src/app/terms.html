WHY THE NSPCC IS SUPPORTING THIS CAMPAIGN

Through the growing evidence from law enforcement agencies, the NSPCC believes there is a strong link between trafficking and slavery, for both children and adults of British and international origin. Through its helpline service the NSPCC is already actively engaged in tackling criminals who exploit vulnerable victims. Through its Child Trafficking Advice Centre (CTAC) the NSPCC has worked hard for many years to support children and young people who have been trafficked.

In addition, the NSPCC works with child protection professionals to recognise the signs of children who are being sexually exploited as well as providing children with therapeutic care through its Protect and Respect service. The NSPCC was approached by the Home Office to provide a helpline service to support its modern slavery campaign, using the current national helpline infrastructure already in place at the NSPCC. This new service is funded by the Home Office and not through NSPCC charitable donations.

2.0

THE ROLE OF THE NSPCC HELPLINE

The modern slavery helpline will be run by the NSPCC helpline team. The Home Office has funded the NSPCC to carry out this service. The NSPCC’s 24/7 national helpline is staffed by practitioners with a child protection background predominantly social workers, teachers and healthcare professionals. The helpline practitioners, who are already trained in recognising the signs of child trafficking have received additional training from the Home Office on how to recognise the signs of modern slavery in adults and children. Calls regarding a child will be handled by the NSPCC’s Child Trafficking Advice Centre. Calls to the helpline can be made anonymously. The helpline will refer information to the police or social services if they believe someone to be in danger or at risk of modern slavery.
